Our Services

Psychotherapy

Evidence-based individual and group therapy — often most powerful when integrated with psychiatric medication care.

Therapy isn't about talking for the sake of talking. It's about building a precise understanding of what's driving your symptoms, then working — deliberately and efficiently — to change it. At Altos, therapy is active, structured, and oriented toward measurable progress. We draw from multiple evidence-based frameworks, matched to the clinical problem, not applied generically.

And because we're a psychiatric practice — not a therapy-only clinic — your therapist works within the same clinical team managing your medication, your measurement data, and your broader treatment plan. That integration changes outcomes.

Core Service

Individual therapy

One-on-one work tailored to your clinical picture — not a one-size-fits-all protocol.

Our individual therapy draws primarily from two complementary traditions. Psychodynamic therapy explores the patterns, relationships, and formative experiences that shape how you think, feel, and respond to the world — often outside your conscious awareness. Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) provides concrete, structured tools to identify and shift the thought patterns and behaviors that maintain symptoms. Most of our work integrates elements of both, because most people benefit from both understanding why and knowing what to do about it.

We also draw from additional modalities when the clinical picture calls for it — including trauma-focused approaches, motivational interviewing, and acceptance-based frameworks. The modality follows the problem. We don't force a clinical situation into a theoretical box.

Approaches

How we work

Different problems require different tools. Here's what we draw from — and when.

Psychodynamic Therapy

Explores recurring relational patterns, unconscious conflicts, and the impact of early experiences on current functioning. Particularly effective for chronic depression, personality difficulties, relational struggles, and conditions where "knowing what to do" hasn't been enough.

Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy

Structured, skills-based approach that targets distorted thinking patterns and avoidance behaviors maintaining symptoms. Strong evidence base for anxiety disorders, OCD, panic disorder, insomnia, and acute depressive episodes where behavioral activation is critical.

Trauma-Focused Approaches

For patients with PTSD, complex trauma, or trauma-related presentations, we incorporate evidence-based trauma processing frameworks — including elements of prolonged exposure, cognitive processing, and somatic awareness — calibrated to the patient's window of tolerance.

Acceptance & Motivational Frameworks

For patients struggling with ambivalence, chronic avoidance, or rigid perfectionism, we draw from acceptance and commitment therapy (ACT) and motivational interviewing — approaches designed to move people from stuck to engaged without relying on willpower alone.

Our Approach

What makes therapy here different

We set clear treatment goals from the start and build in regular checkpoints to assess whether we're making meaningful progress. Therapy at Altos is designed to produce change, not to become a permanent fixture in your schedule. Some patients benefit from longer-term work — and that's fine — but it's a clinical decision we make together, not a default.

When your therapist and prescriber are in the same practice — sharing clinical context, measurement data, and a common treatment framework — decisions are better informed. Your therapist sees how medication changes are landing in session. Your prescriber hears what's emerging in therapy. This is how treatment is supposed to work, and it rarely happens when therapy and medication live in separate silos.

We track symptom measures over time — the same validated scales used in your psychiatric care. This gives both you and your therapist objective data on whether therapy is working, where progress is plateauing, and when it might be time to adjust the approach. Good therapy should show up in the numbers, not just in how the session felt.

Therapeutic approach is selected based on your diagnosis, symptom profile, prior treatment history, and personal preferences — not based on which clinician happens to have an opening. If a CBT-forward approach is what the evidence supports for your presentation, that's what we offer. If deeper psychodynamic work is indicated, we go there. The modality follows the problem.

Integrated Care

Therapy and medication — together

For many psychiatric conditions — including moderate-to-severe depression, anxiety disorders, PTSD, and OCD — the strongest evidence supports combined treatment: psychotherapy and medication working in concert. At Altos, this isn't a referral relationship between two separate practices. It's coordinated care under one roof, with shared clinical context.

If you'd like both therapy and medication here: We can pair you with a therapist and prescriber within our practice who collaborate directly on your care — sharing notes, discussing your progress, and making coordinated treatment decisions.

If you already have a therapist you trust: We're well set up to serve as your medication management partner, coordinating with your outside therapist to ensure your pharmacotherapy and psychotherapy are aligned. We won't duplicate what's already working.

Getting Started

What to expect

Your first sessions focus on understanding your history, current symptoms, what you've tried before, and what you're hoping to change. We collaboratively define treatment goals and discuss which therapeutic approach is best suited to your presentation. This isn't a formality — it's the clinical foundation everything else builds on.

Most patients start with weekly sessions (50 minutes). As symptoms improve and goals are met, we may shift to biweekly or monthly sessions. Session structure varies by modality — CBT sessions tend to be more structured with between-session assignments; psychodynamic sessions allow more open exploration. Both are purposeful.

Every 8–12 sessions, we step back and formally assess progress — reviewing symptom measures, revisiting goals, and discussing whether the current approach is working or needs adjustment. If something isn't moving, we change it. Therapy shouldn't become a habit that substitutes for actual improvement.

Good therapy has an endpoint. When treatment goals are met, we work through a structured termination process — consolidating gains, identifying early warning signs for relapse, building a maintenance plan, and ensuring you leave with the tools and self-awareness to sustain progress independently. If you need to return in the future, we're here — but the goal is to not need to.
