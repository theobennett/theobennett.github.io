Understanding TMS

Your brain has a rhythm — and sometimes it gets stuck

Think about what it feels like when you're having a really great conversation with someone. You're both locked in — finishing each other's thoughts, laughing at the same moments, completely connected. Or think about being on a hike when the light hits just right, the air smells like pine, and for a few minutes you feel completely present and at ease. Or listening to a song that gives you chills.

In all of those moments, different parts of your brain are working together in sync — like musicians in an orchestra all playing in the same key, at the same tempo. There's a natural rhythm to it. Brain scientists call this neural synchrony, and it's what healthy brain function looks and feels like.

When someone has depression, something goes wrong with that rhythm. The parts of the brain that handle mood, motivation, focus, and connection to others fall out of sync with each other. It's like a few instruments in the orchestra started playing in a different key — not because the musicians forgot how to play, but because the conductor lost the beat. The result is that everything feels harder: getting out of bed, caring about things you used to enjoy, connecting with people, thinking clearly. It's not laziness. It's not weakness. It's a brain that has lost its internal timing.

TMS — Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation — is a way to help the conductor find the beat again.

How It Works

Gentle pulses that retune your brain

TMS uses a small device placed against your head that sends brief, gentle magnetic pulses — similar to the technology in an MRI machine, but much more focused and targeted. These pulses pass painlessly through your skull and into a specific area of the brain called the prefrontal cortex — the part responsible for mood regulation, decision-making, and emotional connection.

Each pulse creates a tiny electrical current in that brain area — not enough to feel, but enough to wake up the neurons (brain cells) that have gone quiet during depression. Think of it like gently tapping a tuning fork: you're giving those neurons a reference pitch so they can start vibrating at the right frequency again.

Over a series of sessions, these repeated pulses help the brain rewire itself — strengthening the connections between brain regions that had fallen out of communication. It's not forcing anything artificial into your brain. It's reminding your brain how to do what it already knows how to do — but had forgotten.

The Science

Why does it work?

A bit more detail for those who want to understand the mechanism — still no medical degree required.

Your brain runs on electricity. Every thought, feeling, and memory is produced by tiny electrical signals traveling between billions of neurons (brain cells). These neurons don't work alone — they work in networks, and those networks need to fire together in coordinated patterns to function well.

Research has shown that in people with depression, a specific brain network is underactive: the left dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC). This area is involved in mood regulation, motivation, working memory, and the ability to experience positive emotions. When it's underactive, it's like turning the volume down on the parts of your brain that help you feel engaged with life.

At the same time, another area — the subgenual anterior cingulate cortex — tends to be overactive. This region is associated with rumination, guilt, sadness, and the kind of negative thought loops that characterize depression. So you get a double problem: the "feel engaged" area is too quiet, and the "feel terrible" area is too loud.

TMS sends focused magnetic pulses to the underactive area, stimulating those neurons to fire. Over repeated sessions, this stimulation strengthens the connections within the healthy network and helps restore the balance between the "engagement" circuit and the "rumination" circuit. The brain learns a new (actually, old) pattern — and depression lifts.

This is called neuroplasticity: the brain's ability to rewire itself based on repeated stimulation. TMS doesn't add anything foreign to your brain. It uses your brain's own capacity for change — it just gives it a push in the right direction.

Treatment Options

Two approaches to TMS

We plan to offer both standard TMS and the accelerated SAINT protocol developed at Stanford.

Standard TMS

The established approach. You come in for a 20–40 minute session, 5 days a week, for about 6 weeks (roughly 30–36 sessions total). You sit in a comfortable chair, the device is positioned on your head, and you can read, listen to music, or just relax during treatment. No anesthesia. No recovery time. You drive yourself home and go about your day.

  • FDA-cleared for depression and OCD
  • 6-week course, 5 sessions per week
  • 20–40 minutes per session
  • No sedation, no downtime
  • Response rates of 50–60% in treatment-resistant patients

SAINT Protocol (Stanford Accelerated)

Developed at Stanford University, the SAINT protocol compresses the entire TMS course into just 5 days. Instead of one session per day over 6 weeks, you receive multiple shorter sessions per day for one week. The protocol uses brain imaging (fMRI) to precisely target the exact spot in your prefrontal cortex that needs stimulation — personalized to your brain anatomy.

  • 5-day accelerated treatment course
  • 10 sessions per day, spaced 50 minutes apart
  • fMRI-guided targeting for precision
  • Stanford trial showed 79% remission rate
  • Rapid onset — significant improvement within days, not weeks

The Experience

What does a session feel like?

You'll sit in a padded, reclining chair — similar to a dentist's chair, but more comfortable. The TMS technician places a small device against the left side of your head. When treatment starts, you'll feel a tapping or clicking sensation on your scalp — like someone lightly tapping their finger. It's unusual at first, but most people get used to it within the first few minutes.

There's no sedation, no anesthesia, and no "altered state." You're fully awake and alert the entire time. Many patients listen to music, podcasts, or audiobooks during sessions. Some just close their eyes and rest.

When the session is done, you stand up and leave. No recovery period. No driving restrictions. No cognitive fog. You can go right back to work, exercise, or anything else in your day.

Common side effects: Mild scalp discomfort or headache during or after sessions, especially in the first week. These typically diminish quickly. Serious side effects are extremely rare.

Candidacy

Who is TMS for?

TMS is not a first-line treatment — it's designed for people who have already tried other approaches without getting the relief they need. It's most appropriate for:

  • Treatment-resistant depression — you've tried two or more antidepressants at adequate doses and durations without sufficient improvement
  • Medication intolerance — side effects from antidepressants (weight gain, sexual dysfunction, cognitive blunting) have made it impossible to stay on treatment
  • OCD — FDA-cleared for obsessive-compulsive disorder that hasn't responded adequately to first-line therapies
  • Preference for non-medication approaches — some patients prefer a treatment that doesn't involve daily pills, particularly if they've had difficult experiences with medications
  • Adjunctive treatment — TMS can be used alongside medication and therapy, often enhancing the effectiveness of both

TMS is not appropriate for everyone. People with metal implants in or near the head (excluding standard dental work), a history of seizures, or certain neurological conditions may not be candidates. Candidacy is determined through a thorough clinical evaluation — not self-selection.

FAQ

Common questions about TMS

Most people describe it as a tapping or light knocking sensation on the scalp. It can feel a bit strange or mildly uncomfortable during the first few sessions, but most patients adjust quickly. It's not painful. There's no need for any anesthesia or numbing — you're fully awake and comfortable throughout.

With standard TMS, most patients begin to notice changes around weeks 3–4, with full effects emerging by the end of the 6-week course. Some people notice subtle shifts earlier — sleeping a bit better, feeling slightly more motivated, finding it a little easier to engage with others. With the SAINT protocol, improvement can begin within days due to the concentrated treatment schedule.

TMS has been used safely on hundreds of thousands of patients worldwide since its FDA clearance in 2008. The most common side effects are mild scalp discomfort and headache, which typically resolve within the first week. The most serious — but extremely rare — risk is seizure, occurring in fewer than 1 in 10,000 sessions. TMS does not cause the systemic side effects associated with medications (no weight gain, no sexual dysfunction, no cognitive fog).

Most major insurance plans now cover TMS for treatment-resistant depression, though coverage criteria vary. Typically, your insurer will require documentation that you've tried and not responded to at least two antidepressant medications. We assist with prior authorization and work with your insurance to maximize coverage. We'll discuss cost and coverage options during your consultation before you commit to anything.

For many patients, the benefits of a full TMS course last 6–12 months or longer. Some people experience sustained remission. Others may benefit from occasional "maintenance" sessions (once a month or once a quarter) to keep the gains in place. If symptoms begin to return, a shorter re-treatment course is often effective. TMS results tend to be durable — especially when combined with ongoing therapy and, where appropriate, medication.

They're very different. ECT uses an electrical current to induce a brief seizure under general anesthesia — it's effective but carries risks including memory loss and requires hospital-level resources. TMS uses magnetic pulses (no electrical current enters the brain directly), requires no anesthesia, causes no seizures, and has no cognitive side effects. You walk in, sit down, get treated, and walk out. They're on entirely different ends of the spectrum in terms of intensity, invasiveness, and side effect profile.
